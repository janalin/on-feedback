After a talk, we have

questions,

a scientific debate,

feedback on the talk.

This is about feedback.

This is not about how to ask or answer questions.

This is not about how to have a scientific debate.

 

Yet, guiding principles overlap, of course.

Learn the art of giving feedback in 5 minutes.

This is not possible.

Giving good feedback is almost as difficult as giving good talks.

Let's try instead...

Get started with effective feedback in 5 minutes.

There is always feedback to give.

What makes feedback effective?

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

Concrete means

No "It's nice!" or "I didn't get it!".

Instead, provide a why.

What was nice? Where did you get lost?

 

If you are the presenter, ask for a why.

Be concrete

For instance, distinguish between:

  • Science → content.
  • Structure, language, diagrams, slides → clarity.
  • Presenter → style?
 

Listen actively. Evaluate your impressions.

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

Be concise

Prioritize your feedback. Take into account:

  • the scope of the talk.
  • the speaker's skill level.
 

Avoid repeting others.

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

Be constructive

Add a call to action. For instance:

  • What to keep → "Continue doing..."
  • What to stop → "Remove..."
  • What to start → "Include..."
 

Constructive feedback is both correcting and reinforcing.

Why is giving feedback it worth the hussle?

By giving feedback, you learn to

reflect quickly, think critically, and to identify gaps, jargon, and pitfals.

You'll understand what makes

a logic structure, clear slides, and self-explaining diagrams.

 

Good talks are focused and audience-centric. Giving feedback helps you to understand your audience.

Before we close...

If you receive feedback

Listen. Do not defend yourself. You are free to disagree.

Ask. Was the feedback concrete enough?

Thank. Feedback is a gift.

And I know we all really care for good science and good talks. Yet, ...

On whatever side of the room you are

Be patient. Relax.

Agree to disagree.

 

And remember:

I'm OK.

You're OK.
