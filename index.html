After a talk,

we have questions,

a scientific debate,

feedback on the talk.

This talk is only about feedback.

It's not about how to

  • ask or answer questions.

  • have a scientific debate.

 

Yet, some guidelines overlap, of course.

Learn the art of giving feedback in 10 minutes.

This is not possible.

Giving good feedback is almost as difficult as giving good talks.

Let's try instead...

Get started with effective feedback in 10 minutes.

There is always feedback to give.

What makes feedback effective?

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

Concrete means

No "It's nice!" or "I didn't get it!".

Instead, provide a why.

What was nice? Where did you get lost?

 

If you are the presenter, ask for a why.

Things to try to be concrete...

Differentiate between:

  • Science → content.
  • Structure, diagrams, slides, jargon → clarity.
  • Presenter → style?

Giving concrete feedback can be hard work.

Listen actively.

Evaluate your impressions.

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

Things to try to be concise...

Prioritize your feedback.

Take into account:

  • the scope of the talk.

  • the speaker's skill level.

 

Avoid repeting others.

Respect speaker's and audience's interest.

Some things are better to discuss in person after the session. Some are relevant to everyone.

Effective feedback is

concrete,

concise,

constructive.

What constructive feedback is can be a matter of opinion.

Things to try to be constructive...

Add a call to action.

For instance:

  • What to keep → "Continue doing..."

  • What to stop → "Remove..."

  • What to start → "Include..."

 

Constructive feedback is both correcting and reinforcing.

3 reasons why giving feedback is worth the hussle for you?

1st

It teaches you to

  • think critically,

  • identify gaps and pitfalls,

  • appraise jargon.

2nd

You will better understand what makes

  • a logic structure,

  • clear slides,

  • self-explanatory diagrams.

3rd

Good talks are focused and audience-centric. Giving feedback helps you to understand your audience.

Before we close...

If you receive feedback

Listen. Do not defend yourself. You are free to disagree.

Ask. Was the feedback concrete?

Thank. Feedback is a gift.

And I know we all care for good science and good talks. Yet, ...

On whatever side of the room you are:

  • Be patient. Relax.

  • Learn on what to agree and on what to disagree.

     

  • And remember:

I'm OK.

You're OK.

What you can try to get started:

  1. Collect concrete feedback during the talk.
  2. Filter with respect to redundancy and priority after the talk.
  3. Phrase it as a question or call to action.
